# Scarcity and Urgency

Two different levers, often confused. Hormozi separates them sharply:

- **Scarcity = function of QUANTITY.** "There are only X of these available."
- **Urgency = function of TIME.** "You only have until Y to act."

Both work because of a simple psychology: *fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain.*

The underlying principle Hormozi calls the **Delicate Dance of Desire**:

> "People want what they can't have. People want what other people want. People want things only a select few have access to."

Keep supply *under* the demand you can generate. Always sell out.

---

## Scarcity

### Three Types of Scarcity (general)

1. **Limited Supply of Seats/Slots** — in general, or over a period.
2. **Limited Supply of Bonuses** — the bonuses run out before the core does.
3. **Never Available Again** — this version of the offer is gone after X.

### Three Scarcity Mechanics for Services

For service businesses specifically, Hormozi names three honest, ethical patterns:

#### 1. Total Business Cap

> "My agency only will service twenty-five customers total. Period."

A lifetime cap on clients at this service level. Periodically (every 6-12 months) increase by 10-20%, then re-cap. Best for high-tier service tiers and consulting.

#### 2. Growth Rate Cap

> "We only accept 5 new clients per week, and we already have the first 3 spots taken. I have 6 more calls this week, so you can take the spot or wait for our next open week."

A weekly or monthly cap. Forces a decision in real time and feels honest because it ties to fulfillment capacity.

#### 3. Cohort Cap

> "We take on 100 clients 4 times a year. We open the doors then close them."

Discrete intake windows. Used by most cohort-based courses and accelerators. Creates a natural deadline plus a "this batch or wait" decision.

### Scarcity for Physical Products

- Limited releases on flavors, colors, designs, sizes
- **CRUCIAL RULE: To properly utilize this method, you should always sell out.** Don't over-order. Hormozi suggests selling out roughly once a month is the sweet spot.
- Tell everyone you sold out — this is the social-proof move that compounds the next launch
- Chanel reference: sends 1-2 of each piece per store. Manufactured scarcity that telegraphs prestige.

### Honest Scarcity (the most ethical version)

Just tell the truth about your real capacity.

> "Letting people know you are 81% to capacity in your total business will make people more likely to sign up before they lose the chance."

You don't have to make up scarcity. Most services and products genuinely have capacity constraints — name them.

### Pro Tips

- **Extreme Scarcity:** Sell a very limited supply of 1-on-1 access (DM, email, voice memo, Zoom). Cap at a tiny number, price very high. The exclusivity itself is half the product.
- **Once You're Out, You Can Never Come Back:** Cap your service level *and* say leavers can never return. Makes current clients think very hard before leaving.

## Urgency

Hormozi quote at chapter open: *"Deadlines. Drive. Decisions."*

The last 3% of a campaign's timeline produces 50-60% of its sales. Most people who don't act in the final hours were never going to buy anyway. Don't be afraid to actually enforce the deadline.

### The Four Favorite Urgency Types

#### 1. Cohort-Based Rolling Urgency

> "If you sign up today, I can get you in with our next group that kicks off on Monday. Otherwise you'll have to wait until our next kickoff date."

Always-on, always-rolling. Best for cohort-based programs.

**Juiced version** — even more urgent:
> "I actually had a client who signed up a few weeks ago drop out, so I have an opening for our next cohort that kicks off on Monday…"

#### 2. Rolling Seasonal Urgency

Actual countdowns/promotions tied to seasons, with *real dates* — fake dates destroy credibility. Each season is a new wrapper for the same core service:

- "Our New Year Promotion ends Jan 30!"
- "Our Valentines Lovers Promo Ends Feb 14!"
- "Our Sexy By Spring Special Ends March 31!"
- "Our Fools in Love April Promo Ends April 30!"
- "Our Summer Body Bootcamp Ends June 30!"

Hormozi: **"This is my number one strategy for local businesses."** Same offer, new seasonal wrapper every 4-6 weeks. Each launch feels new.

#### 3. Pricing or Bonus-Based Urgency

Urgency on the *promotion*, not the service.

> "Yes, let's get you started today so you can take advantage of the discount you came in for. I'm not sure how long we will be running it as we change them every 4 weeks or so, and this is one of the better ones we have run in a while."

**Pro tip — Clean Your Pipeline With Every Price Change:** if you're going to raise prices, tell everyone in your pipeline. It cleans dead leads and demonstrates a position of strength. Some will convert immediately to lock in the old price.

#### 4. Exploding Opportunity

Real-world arbitrage opportunities that decay — crypto windows, hot new platforms, exploding job offers, regulatory windows. The urgency is *intrinsic to the situation*, not manufactured.

Emphasize the decay: "By the time everyone else figures this out, the spread is gone."

## Combining Scarcity and Urgency

The two stack well. You can use them together:

- "We only take 100 clients per cohort [scarcity], and this cohort closes Friday [urgency]."
- "Only 3 slots left at this tier [scarcity], price goes up $500 on Sunday [urgency]."

Don't overdo it. Pick one of each and make it real.

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Fake deadlines.** Countdown timers that reset when the page reloads. Prospects spot these instantly and lose trust.
- **Permanent "sales."** A discount that's been running for 6 months teaches buyers there's no urgency.
- **Inflated cap numbers.** "Only 1,000 spots!" when you'd be thrilled with 100 sales. Make the cap real.
- **Scarcity without a reason.** "Only 10 spots" with no explanation feels arbitrary. Tie it to fulfillment capacity.

Honest scarcity and real urgency outperform manufactured versions, and they don't decay your trust over time.
