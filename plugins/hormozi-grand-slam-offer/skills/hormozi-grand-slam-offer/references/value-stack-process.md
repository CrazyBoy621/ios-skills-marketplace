# The Five-Step Value Stack Process

Hormozi's exact process for going from a customer's dream outcome to a finished, bundled, irresistible offer. Run this in order — each step depends on the previous.

## Step 1 — Identify the Dream Outcome

Find what the prospect *actually* wants. Hormozi's framing: *"They want the vacation, not the plane flight."*

The deliverable (a course, a workout plan, weekly meetings) is the plane flight. The dream outcome is the vacation — the *result* in their life.

Test: if you ask "and what does that get you?" and there's still a meaningful answer, you haven't reached the dream outcome yet.

Hormozi's gym example: *"Lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks."* Notice this is: big-enough number + specific timeline. That format is doing two things: maximizing Dream Outcome (the 20 lbs) and minimizing Time Delay (the 6 weeks). The framing of the dream outcome already pre-loads two of the four Value Equation variables.

## Step 2 — List Every Problem

Write down every obstacle, friction, and limiting thought the prospect will encounter, *in the sequence they will experience them*.

**Think about it in insane detail.** Consider what happens immediately before and immediately after they use your product. Walk through their day from waking up to using your thing to evaluating the result.

Every problem maps to one of the four Value Equation buckets:

| Bucket | What the prospect is feeling |
|---|---|
| Dream Outcome | "This won't be financially worth it" / "this isn't really what I want" |
| Likelihood of Achievement | "It won't work for me specifically. External factors will get in my way. I can't stick with it." (most unique/service-specific bucket) |
| Effort & Sacrifice | "This will be too hard. I won't like it. I'll suck at it. It's confusing." |
| Time | "This will take too long. I'm too busy. It won't fit my schedule." |

**Pattern Hormozi uses** — for a weight-loss program, list the major steps in the journey, then bucket the problems for each step:

| Journey step | Problems |
|---|---|
| Buying healthy food | hard, confusing, takes time, expensive, unsustainable, family doesn't eat the same, can't on travel |
| Cooking healthy food | hard, confusing, takes time, expensive, ... |
| Eating healthy food | (same buckets) |
| Exercise regularly | (same buckets) |

In Hormozi's example: 16 core problems × 2-4 sub-problems = 32-64 total problems. **The more problems you find, the more you get to solve.**

Aim for 20+ problems minimum across 4-8 journey stages. Don't filter at this stage — write everything down.

## Step 3 — Reframe Problems as Solutions

Turn each problem into a "how to" statement. Add "How to…" and reverse the problem language.

**Pattern:** *"How to [solve X] so that [specific benefit] (especially [avatar])"*

Examples (the verbatim weight-loss program reframings):

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Buying healthy food is hard, confusing, I won't like it | How to make buying healthy food easy and enjoyable, so that anyone can do it (especially busy moms!) |
| Buying healthy food takes too much time | How to buy healthy food quickly |
| Buying healthy food is expensive | How to buy healthy food for less than your current grocery bill |
| Buying healthy food is unsustainable | How to make buying healthy food take less effort than buying unhealthy food |

Do this for every problem from Step 2. You're now sitting on the seed list of every possible piece of your stack.

## Step 4 — Generate Delivery Vehicles

For each solution, brainstorm *every possible way you could deliver it*. Think divergently — anything you could possibly do.

This is the step Hormozi calls "the most important step in this process."

**The Delivery Cube — apply these six axes to each solution:**

1. **Personal attention level?** 1-on-1, small group, one-to-many
2. **Effort expected from client?** DIY (figure it out themselves) / DWY (Done With You — you teach them) / DFY (Done For You — you do it for them)
3. **If live, what environment?** In-person, phone, email, text, Zoom, chat
4. **If recorded, how do they consume it?** Audio, video, written
5. **How quickly do you reply? On what days/hours?** 24/7, business hours, within 5 min, within 24 hrs
6. **The 10x and 1/10x test:**
   - "If they paid 10x ($X × 10), what would I provide?" — this often reveals premium offerings you'd otherwise never imagine
   - "If they paid 1/10x, how would I make this MORE valuable?" — forces you to find leverage (one-to-many, automation, templates)

The 10x/1/10x test is the most powerful prompt in Step 4. It stretches your imagination in both directions and almost always surfaces creative formats you'd never reach otherwise.

**Solve every perceived problem.** Hormozi's eating-out-guide anecdote: one businesswoman was on the fence because she ate out a lot — adding "a guide to eating healthy at the 20 most common restaurants" closed the sale. Single unresolved objections kill deals. Match each delivery vehicle to a specific objection.

## Step 5 — Trim and Stack

Now you have a long list of possible deliverables. Sort by **cost-to-deliver** (your cost in time/money) vs. **value-to-customer** (their perceived benefit).

**Cut order:**

1. Cut **high cost, low value** items first. (Easy.)
2. Cut **low cost, low value** items second. (Anything generic / commoditized.)
3. Keep **low cost, high value** items — these are the gems. One-to-many assets like checklists, templates, swipe files, recorded trainings live here.
4. Keep **high cost, high value** items — these are the anchors. The DFY component, the 1-on-1 time, the in-person event.

Sort each candidate by asking which Value Equation lever it pulls:

1. Adds to financial / dream-outcome value
2. Increases belief they'll succeed
3. Reduces effort / sacrifice
4. Reduces time delay

**Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum:**
- Easy to sell ↔ hard to fulfill
- Hard to sell ↔ easy to fulfill
- Sweet spot: well-selling AND easy to fulfill

**Hormozi's mantra:** *"Create flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction."* Generate the demand first; *then* optimize the back end.

## Step 6 — Bundle and Name

Group the surviving items into named bundles (apply MAGIC to each — see `magic-naming.md`).

**Bundle format:**

```
Problem → Solution Wording → Sexier Bundle Name (value)
```

Hormozi's worked example (weight loss):

> *Buying food →* How anyone can buy food fast, easy, cheaply → **"Foolproof Bargain Grocery System"** — saves hundreds of dollars per month on food and takes less time than your current shopping routine ($1,000 value)

Each bundle in the final stack should:

1. Solve *all* the perceived problems in that area (not just some)
2. Carry the conviction that this thing is one of a kind
3. Be impossible to compare with the competitor down the street

**Assigning dollar values:** Use defensible reasoning. Three patterns:

- **Standalone equivalent.** "What would a similar component cost if bought separately?" (A 1-hr consult at $300, a 10-week course at $2,000, a template pack at $497.)
- **Hourly rate × time saved.** "We save you 10 hours/month × $200/hr = $2,000."
- **Outcome value.** "If this gets you one extra customer per month at $5,000 LTV…"

Annotate the reasoning in the artifact so the user can defend the number under questioning. Stacks with no reasoning fall apart in the prospect's mind.

**Total stack value should be ~10x the price.** If you're charging $2,000, the stack should add up to $20,000+. That's the "money at a discount" framing.

## The Final Recap (Hormozi's verbatim summary)

- Step #1: We figured out our prospective client's dream outcome.
- Step #2: We listed out all the obstacles they're likely to encounter on their way (our opportunities for value).
- Step #3: We listed all those obstacles as solutions.
- Step #4: We figured out all the different ways we could deliver those solutions.
- Step #5a: We trimmed those ways down to only the things that were highest value and lowest cost to us.
- Step #5b: Put all the bundles together into the ultimate high-value deliverable.
