# Pricing — Charge What It's Worth

Hormozi's pricing chapters argue one main thesis: **most people charge too little, and the act of charging too little destroys value across the entire business.** When in doubt, raise the price.

## The Two Modes: Commodity vs. Differentiated

A commodity is "a product available from many places, prone to purchases based on price instead of value." Commodities are priced by market efficiency — just enough margin to survive.

A Grand Slam Offer is, by definition, *not* a commodity. It cannot be directly compared to competitors. That's what unlocks premium pricing.

If a prospect can mentally line up your offer next to a competitor and pick the cheaper one, you're a commodity. Fix that *first* — by making the offer unique, not by adjusting price.

## Why Charge Premium — The Virtuous Cycle of Price

When you **decrease** price, here's what cascades:

- Decreased emotional investment from clients
- Decreased perceived value
- Decreased client results (because they're not invested → they don't follow through)
- Attracts the worst clients (never satisfied until it's free)
- Destroys margin → can't deliver exceptional service
- Decreased profit → can't invest in systems or staff
- Decreased perceived self-worth and conviction in sales

When you **increase** price, the same cascade runs the other way:

- Increased emotional investment from clients
- Increased perceived value
- Increased client results (skin in the game)
- Attracts the BEST clients (easiest to satisfy, cost less to fulfill)
- Multiplied margin → invest in systems, smart people, customer experience, scale
- Increased profit, perceived value of self, perception of impact, service levels, sales team conviction

These aren't independent levers — they reinforce each other. That's why Hormozi calls it a *cycle*. Choosing one direction is choosing the whole spiral.

## Key Quotations on Price

- **Dan Kennedy:** "There is no strategic benefit to being the second cheapest in the marketplace, but there is for being the most expensive."
- **Dan Kennedy:** "Charge as high a price as you can say out loud without cracking a smile."
- **Warren Buffett:** "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."
- **Hormozi:** "The goal isn't just to be slightly above the market price — the goal is to be so much higher that a consumer thinks to themselves, 'This is so much more expensive, there must be something entirely different going on here.'"

## Higher Price = Higher Perceived Value

The blind wine taste-test (cited by Hormozi): the same wine was poured at three different prices. Tasters consistently rated the higher-priced wine as more enjoyable, even though it was identical to the cheap version.

**Raising prices can directly enhance perceived value.** The price tag is itself a quality signal.

## The Bad Pricing Process Hormozi Mocks

This is what most businesses do — and it's the path to commoditization:

1. Look at the marketplace
2. See what everyone else offers
3. Take the average
4. Go slightly below to remain "competitive"
5. Provide competitors' offer with "a little more"
6. End up at "more for less"

Hormozi's punchline: *"The big secret: those competitors they are copying are dead broke."*

## Price–Value Discrepancy

As long as **perceived value > price**, customers stay. The moment value dips below price, you hit the **Point of Cancellation** — they churn.

Goal: create a huge gap between perceived value and price, but do it by *raising the value*, not by lowering the price. This is the "money at a discount" framing: sell $100,000 of value for $10,000.

## How to Set the Price

There's no formula in the book for *exactly* what to charge — Hormozi is allergic to that. But here's the operating logic:

1. **Build the stack first.** Get the bundle total value to ~10x what you intend to charge.
2. **Charge as high as you can say out loud without cracking up.** If you can say "$5,000" with a straight face but $10,000 makes you laugh, the right price is somewhere between.
3. **Test by raising.** If your close rate doesn't drop dramatically, raise more.
4. **Niche down to charge up.** Same product, more specific avatar = 10-100x price. (Hormozi's Dan Kennedy example: a generic time-management course is $19. *"Time Management For Outbound B2B Power Tools & Gardening Sales Reps"* is $1,997. Same course content, 100x the price.)

## Common Pricing Mistakes

- **Anchoring to competitors.** If competitors are commoditized, copying them locks you into commodity pricing.
- **Volume-based pricing.** "I'll just sell more at a lower price" usually means more bad clients, more support burden, and worse fulfillment quality.
- **Apologetic pricing.** Saying the price hesitantly. Hormozi: charge what you can say out loud, then say it out loud.
- **Discounting the core.** Never discount the main offer — it teaches buyers your prices are negotiable. Add bonuses instead.
- **Pricing for the cheapest possible buyer.** That buyer will be your worst client. Price for the buyer you want.

## When the User's Price Seems Low

If a user says "I charge $97/month for my course" and the course has 10+ hours of content, templates, and 1-on-1 support, your job is to push back. The book is unambiguous: most people undercharge.

Push back format:
1. Acknowledge the deliverable
2. Calculate the value at standalone equivalents
3. Show the multiple-they-could-charge-at
4. Give a specific recommendation

Example response:
> "$97/mo is leaving money on the table. The deliverable is doing what would cost ~$3,000 standalone (10 hours of content × $200/hr equivalent + templates worth $497 + 1-on-1 support that goes for $300/session). I'd test at $497/mo or $1,997 one-time, and stack a guarantee. The math: if you close 30% as many people at 5x the price, you're at 1.5x revenue with half the support load."

Be specific, show the math, and propose a number.

## Prerequisite Definitions

- **Gross Profit** = Revenue − direct cost of servicing an *additional* customer.
- **Lifetime Value (LTV / LTGP)** = gross profit accrued over the entire lifetime of a customer.

LTV is what matters — not the upfront price alone — when judging whether a price works.
