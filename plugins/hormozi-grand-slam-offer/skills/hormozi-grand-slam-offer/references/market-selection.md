# Market Selection — The Starving Crowd

The marketing-professor parable: asked what one advantage he'd want for a hot dog stand, the right answer is **"a starving crowd."** A great market beats a great offer beats great persuasion skills.

## The Hierarchy

> **Starving Crowd (market) > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills**

A "great" on a higher-priority lever overrides anything lower. A "bad" lower-priority lever can be saved by a "great" higher-priority lever.

This means: **if the market is bad, no offer fixes it.** If the user's market is dying or non-existent, your job is to say so before stacking value.

## The Four Indicators of a Good Market

A market needs to score well on all four:

### 1. Massive Pain

The audience must not want, but desperately *need* what you're offering. Pain is proportional to the price you can charge.

Hormozi's mantra: **"The pain is the pitch."**

If the prospect feels mild discomfort, you'll get mild prices and mild conversions. If the prospect feels acute pain, they pay almost anything for relief.

### 2. Purchasing Power

The audience must be able to afford the service at the price you require.

Hormozi's negative example: a resume-improvement service that failed because the targets were all *unemployed*. They had the pain and the need — but no money.

Check: does this audience have $X to spend on this category? If your price is $5,000 and your audience is broke students, the offer doesn't matter.

### 3. Easy to Target

There must be associations, mailing lists, social-media groups, communities, or channels where the avatar gathers. If you can't reach them, you can't sell to them.

> "If our potential customers are all gathered together somewhere, then we can market to them."

Test: can you name 3-5 specific channels (a subreddit, a conference, a magazine, an industry association, a Facebook group) where your avatar is concentrated?

### 4. Growing

Growing markets are tailwinds. Declining markets (Hormozi's "Lloyd's newspaper-software" example) are headwinds no offer can overcome.

If the market is shrinking, you're swimming upstream forever — every quarter you have to work harder for the same revenue. Avoid.

## The Three Timeless Macro-Markets

There is always demand for solutions to these core human pains:

- **Health**
- **Wealth**
- **Relationships**

Almost every viable niche sits inside one of these. Find a smaller subgroup within one of them that satisfies the other three indicators.

## Three Market-Quality Buckets

| Market Quality | What Wins | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Great market** | Even a bad offer + bad persuasion works | Toilet paper in early COVID, hot dogs at 2am bar close |
| **Normal market** | Grand Slam Offer makes you rich even with mediocre persuasion | Most people; this is who the book is written for |
| **Bad/dying market** | Nothing works. Avoid. | Newspaper software, fax-only services |

The book assumes you're in at least a normal market. The Grand Slam Offer framework doesn't fix a dying market — it lets you dominate a normal one.

## "Don't Make Me Niche Slap You"

Hormozi's coined warning: don't hop from niche to niche assuming the market is the problem. Pick one, commit, fail until you succeed.

> "The riches are in the niches."

The classic example (from Dan Kennedy): a generic time-management course is $19. Rename it *"Time Management For Outbound B2B Power Tools & Gardening Sales Reps"* and charge $1,997 — same content, 100x the price.

The mechanism: when the avatar is specific, the prospect feels "this is for me, not for everyone." That precision is what justifies premium pricing.

## When to Broaden

(Author's note in the book.) For most operators under $10M/year, niching down makes more money. Only broaden when your TAM (total addressable market) caps you and you're saturating it.

## Applying This in the Skill

When a user describes their market or avatar:

1. **Test against the four indicators.** Score each one. If any are red flags, raise them gently but clearly.
2. **Test the specificity.** If the avatar is "small business owners" or "moms," push for niching down — at minimum geography, industry, or life-stage.
3. **Confirm the macro-market.** Is this health, wealth, or relationships? If not, ask why — most successful niches reduce to one of these.
4. **Validate purchasing power.** If the price you're considering exceeds what this avatar typically spends in this category, flag it.
5. **Check growth.** "Is this market growing, flat, or declining?" If they don't know, suggest they check Google Trends, industry reports, or a few minutes of research.

If the market scores well on all four indicators, proceed with the offer build. If not, fix the market first.
